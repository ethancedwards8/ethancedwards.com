## Home

This is the third time I've been back home since starting my freshman year at
Harvard. In the six months that have passed since I left, not a lot has
physically changed in Hillsville/Carroll County: we have a new store, some
businesses have moved buildings, some trees are gone, and there are new benches
in front of the historic courthouse. That's about it. Oh, and we have a new
stoplight in the middle of town. Big deal.

On the other hand, I've changed a lot. I've traveled across the country
interviewing at different companies, met brilliant people from around the
world, taken graduate-level Computer Science classes, and read some pretty
weird philosophers, and even sent a satellite into space with a club. I've also
adjusted to living in a city as opposed to a small town in Appalachia. I've
lived a lot of life in the few months that I've been away from home, and
learned even more. I've had some pretty defining successes, but also failed
some, too.

None of this is too surprising. I knew going to Harvard would challenge me and
open a lot of doors that didn't exist in Southwest Virginia. But what I did not
expect were the small changes: I'm now more aware of the southern accent that
used to be part of normal life, my friends are getting married, having kids,
and moving away, and teachers, administrators, and staff at school that I once
knew are gone and are replaced with new faces. Annual events like banquets,
conferences, clubs, and sports seasons that were once so important to me kept
happening but with different people.

## Spring Break

I was lucky enough to be able to come home for Spring Break this year. Each
time I come home, I make it a point to visit the high school on my first full
day back and visit some of my old teachers. While it has only been a year,
thankfully, none of my teachers have retired yet. That would make me feel old.

I also try to go running with a few of my close friends from high school Cross
Country, for old time's sake. After, we go eat at a restaurant in town. There
are a few places around the county that I frequented in high school, and it is
nice to revisit. My brother started swimming this year, so I force him to go
swim with me too.

For the most part, I try to settle back into my old school routine when I'm
home. Wake up early, exercise before or after school hours, and attempt to be
at least halfway productive. I guess it is nice to pretend I still live here.

The most obvious affront to this facade occurred when I went to go early vote
in the county complex. Last June, I volunteered as an election officer during a
state primary. When I walked into the office to cast my ballot, I was greeted
with familiar faces and a few hugs, as well as the dreaded question: 'So when
are you coming back to Carroll County?'

There's a small restaurant down our road called Crooked Oak which my family
frequents. Every time we eat there, we see someone we know from the community,
like an old baseball coach, the family of my old podcasting partner, or some
old teachers. This most recent time, I saw an old family friend who asked the
same question: 'When are you coming back home?'

So it has been quite an adjustment. I love my home, its culture, history, and
people. Maybe it's just a small town thing, but I think this place is pretty
special. Even now, I'm writing this while sitting in the back of the car,
riding to my favorite (only) Mexican restaurant in town.

After I voted, I went to a new coffee place nestled on the corner of the
highway. I've never been a coffee person, but college has forced me to start
supplementing my diet with some form of caffeine, in this case matcha. After
walking out, I took a minute to think about how I don't live here anymore.
