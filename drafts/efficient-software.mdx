## Linux

One of the reasons I started using Linux when I was in middle school was my
fascination with how resource efficient Linux was compared to Windows.

Some of this fascination was fueled by the desire to see my RAM usage when
running `neofetch` drop lower and lower on fully-equipped Linux installation.
The lowest I reached was ~80MB on a minimal Gentoo system. While this was fun
and taught me a ton, it certainly wasn't very practical.

The other, more practical, reason was that I had crappy hardware. While I had
built a pretty nice (for the year and the budget) custom gaming PC a few years
prior to starting to use Linux, it took me some time to build up the courage to
install Linux on it. That meant most of my Linux experimentation took place on
older HP laptop with 16GB of RAM, a slow CPU, and an even slower hard drive.

Compounding on all of this was my terrible internet connection. Given that I
lived in the middle of nowhere, the only ISP in our region provided ADSL with
20mb download and 2mb upload. One was lucky to finish a video download in an hour,
let alone anything large like Windows Updates.

I faithfully and joyously used that old HP laptop for a few years, and I still
have it back home. The device saw Ubuntu, Arch, Gentoo, back to Arch, and then
finally NixOS where it rests peacefully today. I really loved that laptop.

Eventually, StarLink was released and I put Linux on my gaming PC, but the
early days of my Linux journey were very formative in how I view computing and
software as a whole. I cherish that time and everything it taught me.

Among the lessons that I learned was that selecting what software a person runs
is really important, for both philosophical and practical reasons. Due to the
limitations of my hardware, I often elected to install and use very minimal for
pragmatic reasons like not wanting to wait for a web browser to load GMail. My
workflows naturally converged to using terminal tools like `neomutt`, `neovim`,
`tmux`, and the [suckless](https://suckless.org) software suite.

## Nix

When I discovered NixOS after around a year of using Linux full time on all of
my personal computing devices, I was amazed, like many others, by the ability
to declaratively manage my system and development environments. I immediately
reached "nixvana".

That being said, one of the unfortunate tradeoffs that Nix makes is that Nix
systems often aren't very minimal or efficient. For example, due to some quirks
with how Nix guarantees reproducibility and determinism, small changes in how a
package recipe (a derivation) is written may invalidate the package's cached
build even if the resulting binary is identical to before.

Another example is the inclusion of superfluous dependencies into a packages's
closure. For a contrived example, let's say that some C++ statistics library a
requires the `cudaPackages.cuda_cudart` library. If within the Nixpkgs
derivation for the statistics library any extra cuda libraries are included,
Nix will automatically realize/download/add the additional dependencies to the
package's closure even if they are not strictly needed. Unfortunately, this is
somewhat common (especially with cuda) in Nixpkgs and it is often hard to
detect.

Another efficiency quirk is Nix's idea of evaluation vs builds. To keep it
short, evaluation creates a `.drv` file that contains the instructions for a
build to happen.

Builds are easily cacheable. There even exists a binary cache for Nixpkgs not
unlike other Linux distributions that distribute binaries to users. But caching
evaluation is much harder, for various reasons that I will not describe here.

## Efficiency

In each of the previous cases, there is some computational waste that occurs.
The way I think about computational waste is mainly in the form of electricity,
but also opportunity cost for users.

If a user has to download extra gigabytes of packages not required to run their
program, they may have to wait a long time before starting their next task,
which has economic and social impacts. But there's also the electrical cost: in
order for a file to be served over the internet from a server in the
Netherlands to a user in the United States, a packet has to be transmitted from
the device through a series of interconnected routers, switches, access points,
servers, and more (this idea is what made me fall in love with networking).

Each time a packet crosses the atlantic, it requires electricity through each
step/hop/route. Of course, millions of engineering hours have went into
compressing the margin of electricity used close to zero, but there is still a
cost. The cost of one packet may be immeasurably small, but what about
trillions every day? I don't know the math, but I'm sure it's greater than $0.

This isn't to say that we shouldn't download software because there is waste
involved. Of course there is. We can thank the second law of thermodynamics for
that. But I am saying that we should think about ways we can reduce waste.

Of course, this thought experiment is by no means limited to the idea of
networking electrical costs. Compiling software is often wasteful, and so is
doing machine learning training runs, serving inference, processing photos,
sending messages, etc. This doesn't make doing any of these things wrong,
but I think it's important to keep in mind.

Everyday users are often insulated from most of the costs in the networking
example, as they typically only pay for what they use on their property.
However, users pay in another way: the environment. Especially as much of this
electricity is being generated from non-renewable and dirty resources, our
planet feels the damage more each year.

## Nix Efforts and Software Excellency

Amazingly, there's been a concerted effort to make Nix more efficient in a
myriad of ways. I am both proud and thankful to have been a part of these
efforts in varying degrees of capacity. These efforts vary from rewriting
complex parts of the internal standard library to removing lockfiles (which
increase the tarball download size) from the Nixpkgs repository and replacing
them with Fixed-Output Derivations.

For one, Nixpkgs contributor [Eman "Llakala" Resu](https://github.com/llakala)
has been working on various evaluation performance increases for Nixpkgs.
Faster evaluation means less CPU time spent on evaluating the package set,
which means less electricity is used. This is important because Nixpkgs is
evaluated hundreds of trillions of times a day by users across the world. This
adds up over time.

I'm also proud to mention some of my (albeit at this time limited) work as a
member of the Nixpkgs CUDA team to remove unused CUDA dependencies from
packages that do not require them. Some of my changes have resulted in as much
as 3 Gbs being removed from a package's closure. It's not much, but it's honest
work.

There are also various Nix knobs that help reduce the amount of computational
waste. Unfortunately, many of these like `outputs` or `strictDeps` are disabled
or unset by default in Nixpkgs, but we're getting there one step at a time.

I'm currently writing a script for package maintainers to use that gives them
recommendations on how to improve their packages to enable these options. A
nice side effect of this effort is that packages in Nixpkgs that may not have
been edited in many years are being modernized.

Another way I think we can avoid waste is by writing excellent, minimal
programs. Software Engineering is a technical skill, but it is also an art
and a craft that can be learned and taught. 

High quality software that aspires to be accessible to and usable by everyone
regardless of where they live or what kind of hardware they have is incredibly
important.
